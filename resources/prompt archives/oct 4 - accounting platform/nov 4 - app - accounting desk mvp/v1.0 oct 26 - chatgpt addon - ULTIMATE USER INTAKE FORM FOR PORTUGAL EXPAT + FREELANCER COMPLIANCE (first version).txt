To build a first‑version intake wizard that is genuinely idiot‑proof, accessible on mobile, and pleasing even for distracted or non‑tech‑savvy users, you should think in terms of small, clear steps and progressive disclosure rather than a single intimidating form. Here’s how I would structure it and what to include or defer for later iterations:

1. Break the journey into 4–5 steps with a progress bar

A multi‑step wizard reduces cognitive load, lets users focus on one theme at a time, and gives them dopamine hits as they complete each step. Each step should have no more than 4–5 fields and a clear heading. For example:

Basics & Contact – name, email, country, preferred language. Explain in one line why you need this (“so we can send your summary and contact you if needed”).

Identity & Residence – passport upload, date of birth, NIF, visa type, current address, lease registered (yes/no).

Activity & Income – have you opened activity? CAE code (dropdown with common options), start date, expected turnover, invoicing platform. Show VAT questions only if they enter an estimate > €14 500 for 2024 or > €15 000 for 2025
support.rauva.com
.

Social Security & Tax History – NISS status (have/need), prior IRS filings, any Finanças letters or fines, fiscal‑representative status
portugalhomes.com
.

Readiness & Next Steps – urgency (drop‑down), whether they have an accountant, open comments (“Describe your biggest worry”), consent checkbox for data use.

A progress indicator (“Step 2 of 5”) reassures users they’re advancing. Allow back/forward navigation so they can correct errors without starting over.

2. No login for the first version

Logins are friction. For the MVP, collect essential contact info and let users complete the form in one go. Save their submission to Supabase with a unique ID and send a confirmation email with a link to update details if needed. Once the model is proven, you can offer optional accounts for users who want to track status or manage multiple clients.

3. Success page with clear expectations, not over‑promising

After submission, show a friendly thank‑you page that:

Confirms you’ve received their information.

Tells them when to expect their personalised summary (e.g., “within 2 business days”).

Explains what happens next (e.g., you’ll review their documents and, if they opted for help, match them with a certified accountant).

Offers a call‑to‑action to join your newsletter or early‑access program.

Include an automatic email receipt with the same information. For a future iteration, you can attach an auto‑generated PDF summary using a language model API, but don’t delay the MVP to integrate AI. Focus first on shipping the form and sending a plain‑text summary.

4. Make it mobile‑first and accessible

Big inputs and simple language: use large tap targets, plain EN/PT labels, and avoid bureaucratic terms (“freelance activity start” instead of “Início de Atividade”).

Auto‑save progress: store each step in local storage so a user can resume if they accidentally close the tab.

Conditional logic: hide fields that don’t apply (e.g., VAT questions if turnover is below the threshold).

Upload helpers: accept photos as well as PDFs for passport and lease uploads, and show immediate validation (e.g., reject non‑PT IBAN formats).

Accessibility: ensure high contrast, large fonts, and screen‑reader labels; avoid long scrolling pages.

5. Build with the future in mind, but don’t over‑engineer

Supabase structure: store each submission in an intakes table, with JSON for step data and tags for quick filtering. Add a simple users table later when you introduce logins.

AI summary: once submissions start flowing, you can generate a PDF summary using OpenAI or Gemini. For the MVP, just send a plain summary by email; the AI integration can be an upgrade.

Dashboard for accountants: that will be Phase 2 or 3. Focus first on collecting high‑quality intake data. When ready, allow accountants to sign up, pay, and view anonymised leads.

6. Make it feel premium

A minimalist, premium feel comes from uncluttered design and thoughtful micro‑copy, not necessarily from fancy graphics. Use lots of white space, clear typography, and a consistent colour palette. Offer quick tips or tooltips (e.g., “Your CAE code is usually on your business registration document”) and success animations when a step is completed. These touches keep users engaged without overwhelming them.

Bottom line: start with a simple, multi‑step, mobile‑friendly wizard that collects contact info, verifies identity and residence details, captures activity and tax status, and asks about urgency and history. Don’t require logins or complicated features up front. Send a confirmation email and let them know what happens next. Once you’ve proven people will fill this out, you can add AI‑generated summaries, user accounts, and an accountant dashboard.
